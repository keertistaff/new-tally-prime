Introduction

Before learning voucher entries in TallyPrime, it is important to understand the Concept of Accounting.

TallyPrime is accounting software, but accounting itself is a system of recording, classifying, summarizing, and reporting financial transactions of a business.

Every transaction entered in TallyPrime follows accounting principles and rules.

What is Accounting?

Accounting is the process of:

  1. Recording business transactions
  2. Classifying transactions
  3. Summarizing financial data
  4. Preparing reports
  5. Helping management make decisions

Simple Definition

Accounting is the language of business.

It tells:

  • How much money business owns
  • How much business owes
  • How much profit is earned
  • How much loss is incurred

Real Life Example

Imagine you open a mobile accessories shop.

Date Transaction
1 Jan Started business with ₹5,00,000
2 Jan Purchased goods ₹1,00,000
5 Jan Sold goods ₹20,000
10 Jan Paid rent ₹10,000

Without accounting:

  • You cannot know profit.
  • You cannot know stock value.
  • You cannot know cash balance.

With accounting:

Everything is recorded and reported.

This is where TallyPrime helps.

Objectives of Accounting

Accounting helps to:

1. Record Transactions

Every transaction is recorded systematically.

Example: Purchased Laptop ₹50,000.

2. Know Profit or Loss

Business owner can know:

  • Monthly profit
  • Quarterly profit
  • Yearly profit

Example:

Sales = ₹10,00,000

Expenses = ₹8,00,000

Profit = ₹2,00,000

3. Know Financial Position

Business can know:

  • Assets
  • Liabilities
  • Capital

Through Balance Sheet.

4. Compliance with Laws

Accounting helps in:

  • GST Return
  • Income Tax Return
  • Audit
  • TDS

5. Decision Making

Example:

A shop owner notices one product gives higher profit.

He decides to stock more of that item.

Accounting provides this information.

Basic Accounting Terminology

Before using TallyPrime, understand these terms.

1. Business Transaction

Any transaction involving money and affecting business is called a business transaction.

Examples

  • Purchased goods
  • Sold goods
  • Paid rent
  • Received payment

Not Business Transaction

Owner bought groceries for home.

This is a personal transaction.

2. Capital

Money invested by owner in business.

Example

Mr. Raj starts business with ₹10,00,000.

Capital = ₹10,00,000

Tally Entry

Cash A/c Dr ₹10,00,000
To Capital A/c ₹10,00,000

Real Life Practice

When a new company is created in TallyPrime, the opening investment of owner is recorded as Capital.

3. Drawings

Money withdrawn by owner for personal use.

Example

Owner takes ₹20,000 for family expenses.

Tally Entry

Drawings A/c Dr ₹20,000
To Cash A/c ₹20,000

Real Life Practice

Business owners often withdraw money for:

  • Home expenses
  • School fees
  • Personal travel

These should never be recorded as business expenses.

4. Assets

Assets are things owned by business having value.

Examples

  • Cash
  • Bank Balance
  • Furniture
  • Machinery
  • Computers
  • Stock
  • Building

Real Life Example

A retail shop owns:

  • Cash ₹50,000
  • Stock ₹2,00,000
  • Furniture ₹30,000

Total Assets = ₹2,80,000

Types of Assets

Current Assets

Can be converted into cash quickly.

  • Cash
  • Bank
  • Debtors
  • Stock

Fixed Assets

Used for long-term business operations.

  • Building
  • Machinery
  • Furniture
  • Computer

TallyPrime Practice

  • Cash → Current Asset
  • Bank → Bank Account
  • Furniture → Fixed Asset

5. Liabilities

Liabilities are obligations of business.

Money payable by business.

Examples

  • Loan
  • Creditors
  • Outstanding Expenses

Real Life Example

Business purchased goods on credit from supplier ₹1,00,000.

Supplier becomes creditor.

Business owes money.

Therefore: ₹1,00,000 = Liability

Types of Liabilities

Current Liabilities

  • Creditors
  • GST Payable
  • Outstanding Salary

Long-Term Liabilities

  • Bank Loan
  • Vehicle Loan

TallyPrime Practice

Creditors ledger group: Sundry Creditors

Loan ledger group: Loans (Liability)

6. Income

Money earned from business activities.

  • Sales
  • Commission Received
  • Interest Received
  • Service Charges

Real Life Example

A coaching institute receives ₹50,000 as tuition fees.

This is income.

TallyPrime Practice

  • Sales Ledger
  • Indirect Income Ledger
  • Commission Received Ledger

7. Expenses

Expenses are costs incurred to earn income.

  • Salary
  • Rent
  • Electricity
  • Telephone
  • Fuel
  • Internet

Real Life Example

A company pays:

  • Rent ₹20,000
  • Salary ₹50,000

Total Expense = ₹70,000

TallyPrime Practice

Create expense ledgers under Direct Expenses or Indirect Expenses.

8. Debtors

Customers who owe money to business.

Real Life Example

Goods sold on credit to Amit for ₹25,000.

Amit has not paid yet.

Amit becomes Debtor.

Tally Entry

Amit A/c Dr ₹25,000
To Sales A/c ₹25,000

Real Life Practice

  • Follow up payments
  • Reduce bad debts
  • Improve cash flow

9. Creditors

Suppliers to whom business owes money.

Real Life Example

Purchased goods on credit from XYZ Traders ₹50,000.

XYZ Traders becomes creditor.

Tally Entry

Purchase A/c Dr ₹50,000
To XYZ Traders A/c ₹50,000

Real Life Practice

Businesses maintain creditor schedules for payment planning.

Accounting Equation

Every business transaction follows:

Assets = Capital + Liabilities

This is the foundation of accounting.

Example 1

Assets: Cash = ₹5,00,000

Capital: Owner Investment = ₹5,00,000

₹5,00,000 = ₹5,00,000 + ₹0

Balanced.

Example 2

Business takes bank loan ₹2,00,000.

Assets: Cash = ₹7,00,000

Capital = ₹5,00,000

Liability = ₹2,00,000

₹7,00,000 = ₹5,00,000 + ₹2,00,000

Golden Rules of Accounting

1. Personal Account

Debit the Receiver

Credit the Giver

Rahul A/c Dr ₹10,000
To Cash A/c ₹10,000

2. Real Account

Debit What Comes In

Credit What Goes Out

Furniture A/c Dr ₹30,000
To Cash A/c ₹30,000

3. Nominal Account

Debit All Expenses and Losses

Credit All Incomes and Gains

Rent A/c Dr ₹10,000
To Cash A/c ₹10,000

Double Entry System

Every transaction affects at least two accounts.

This is called Double Entry Accounting.

Example

Computer A/c Dr ₹40,000
To Cash A/c ₹40,000

Affected Accounts:

  1. Computer increases
  2. Cash decreases

Why TallyPrime Uses Double Entry

  • Detect errors
  • Maintain accuracy
  • Generate reports automatically

  • Cash Flow
  • GST Liability

Conclusion

The Concept of Accounting is the foundation of TallyPrime. Every voucher, ledger, inventory transaction, GST entry, receipt, payment, purchase, and sale recorded in TallyPrime follows accounting principles such as Capital, Assets, Liabilities, Income, Expenses, Double Entry System, and the Accounting Equation.

A strong understanding of these concepts makes it much easier to learn advanced TallyPrime topics like Voucher Entry, GST Accounting, Inventory Management, Payroll, Cost Centres, and Financial Reporting.
