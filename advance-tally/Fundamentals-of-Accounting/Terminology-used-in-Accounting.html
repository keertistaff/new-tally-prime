1. What is Accounting?

Accounting is the systematic process of recording, classifying, summarizing, analyzing, and interpreting financial transactions of a business.

Simple Definition

Accounting is the language of business. It tells us:

  • How much money a business owns
  • How much it owes
  • How much profit or loss it earns
  • Its financial position

Real-Life Example

Suppose Rahul starts a mobile shop.

  • Invests ₹5,00,000
  • Buys mobiles worth ₹3,00,000
  • Sells mobiles for ₹4,00,000

Accounting records all these transactions and shows whether Rahul earned a profit.

2. Basic Accounting Terminologies

A. Business Transaction

Meaning

Any financial activity that affects the financial position of a business.

Examples

  • Buying goods
  • Selling products
  • Paying salary
  • Receiving rent

Real Practice

A supermarket purchases inventory worth ₹50,000.

This is a business transaction because money and goods are involved.

B. Capital

Meaning

Amount invested by the owner into the business.

Formula

Capital = Owner's Investment

Example

Priya starts a boutique by investing ₹10,00,000.

Capital = ₹10,00,000

Real-World Practice

When founders invest money into a startup, it is recorded as capital.

C. Drawings

Meaning

Money or goods withdrawn by the owner for personal use.

Example

Owner withdraws ₹20,000 from business for household expenses.

Drawings = ₹20,000

Real-Life Example

A restaurant owner uses business cash to pay personal school fees.

This amount is treated as drawings.

D. Assets

Meaning

Resources owned by a business that provide future benefits.

Examples

  • Cash
  • Furniture
  • Building
  • Machinery
  • Inventory
  • Vehicles

Formula

Assets = Liabilities + Capital

Real-World Example

A manufacturing company owns:

  • Plant = ₹50 lakh
  • Machinery = ₹20 lakh
  • Cash = ₹10 lakh

These are all assets.

Types of Assets

1. Current Assets

Assets convertible into cash within one year.

Examples

  • Cash
  • Bank balance
  • Inventory
  • Debtors

Example

A grocery store's stock is sold within a few weeks.

Thus inventory is a current asset.

2. Non-Current Assets (Fixed Assets)

Long-term assets used in business operations.

Examples

  • Land
  • Building
  • Machinery
  • Vehicles

Example

A factory machine used for 10 years is a fixed asset.

E. Liabilities

Meaning

Amounts owed by the business to outsiders.

Examples

  • Bank loans
  • Creditors
  • Outstanding expenses

Real-Life Example

A company borrows ₹5 lakh from a bank.

This becomes a liability.

Types of Liabilities

1. Current Liabilities

Payable within one year.

Examples

  • Creditors
  • Outstanding wages
  • Taxes payable

Example

Electricity bill unpaid at year-end.

Current liability.

2. Long-Term Liabilities

Payable after one year.

Examples

  • Home loan
  • Bank loan
  • Debentures

Example

A company takes a 5-year loan for machinery.

Long-term liability.

F. Goods

Meaning

Items purchased for resale.

Example

For a mobile shop:

  • Mobiles = Goods
  • Furniture = Not Goods

Real Practice

In a clothing store:

  • Shirts = Goods
  • Cash counter = Asset

G. Purchases

Meaning

Goods bought for resale.

Example

A retailer buys inventory worth ₹1,00,000.

Purchases = ₹1,00,000

Real-Life Practice

Amazon sellers regularly purchase products for resale.

H. Sales

Meaning

Goods sold to customers.

Example

Goods sold worth ₹1,50,000.

Sales = ₹1,50,000

Real Example

A bakery sells cakes worth ₹20,000 daily.

These sales generate revenue.

I. Revenue

Meaning

Income generated from normal business operations.

Examples

  • Sales revenue
  • Service revenue
  • Commission income

Real Practice

A consulting company earns ₹2 lakh from client services.

Revenue = ₹2 lakh

J. Expenses

Meaning

Costs incurred to earn revenue.

Examples

  • Salary
  • Rent
  • Electricity
  • Advertising

Real-Life Example

A company spends ₹50,000 on employee salaries.

This is an expense.

K. Income

Meaning

Increase in economic benefits.

Examples

  • Interest received
  • Commission received
  • Rent received

Example

Company earns ₹10,000 interest from bank deposits.

Income = ₹10,000

L. Profit

Meaning

Excess of revenue over expenses.

Formula

Profit = Revenue − Expenses

Example

Revenue = ₹5,00,000

Expenses = ₹3,50,000

Profit = ₹1,50,000

Real-Life Example

A café earns:

  • Sales = ₹8 lakh
  • Expenses = ₹6 lakh

Profit = ₹2 lakh

M. Loss

Meaning

Expenses exceed revenue.

Formula

Loss = Expenses − Revenue

Example

Revenue = ₹2 lakh

Expenses = ₹3 lakh

Loss = ₹1 lakh

Real Example

Many startups incur losses in their initial years.

3. Debtor

Meaning

Person who owes money to the business.

Example

Goods sold on credit to Amit worth ₹25,000.

Amit becomes a debtor.

Real Practice

Wholesale distributors often sell goods on 30-day credit.

Customers become debtors until payment is received.

4. Creditor

Meaning

Person to whom the business owes money.

Example

Goods purchased on credit from Raj worth ₹50,000.

Raj becomes a creditor.

Real-Life Practice

Retail stores buy inventory from suppliers on credit.

Suppliers are creditors.

5. Receivables

Meaning

Amounts to be received from customers.

Example

Customer owes ₹40,000.

Receivable = ₹40,000

6. Payables

Meaning

Amounts to be paid to suppliers.

Example

Business owes ₹70,000 to a supplier.

Payable = ₹70,000

7. Inventory (Stock)

Meaning

Goods available for sale.

Example

A shoe store has 500 pairs of shoes.

Inventory = Unsold shoes.

Real Practice

Large companies maintain inventory records daily.

8. Cash Discount

Meaning

Discount given for early payment.

Example

Bill = ₹10,000

Discount = ₹500

Customer pays = ₹9,500

Real-Life Example

Suppliers often offer:

“2% discount if payment made within 10 days.”

9. Trade Discount

Meaning

Discount given at the time of sale.

Example

MRP = ₹50,000

Trade Discount = 10%

Selling Price = ₹45,000

Real Practice

Electronic dealers receive trade discounts from manufacturers.

10. Bad Debts

Meaning

Amount not recoverable from customers.

Example

Customer owes ₹20,000 and becomes insolvent.

Bad Debt = ₹20,000

Real-Life Example

Banks classify certain loans as bad debts when borrowers fail to repay.

11. Depreciation

Meaning

Reduction in value of an asset due to use, wear and tear, or obsolescence.

Example

Machine Cost = ₹1,00,000

Annual Depreciation = ₹10,000

Book Value after 1 year = ₹90,000

Real Practice

Every company depreciates:

  • Machinery
  • Vehicles
  • Computers

12. Accounting Equation

This is the foundation of accounting.

Assets = Liabilities + Capital

Example

Assets = ₹10,00,000

Liabilities = ₹4,00,000

Capital = ₹6,00,000

Therefore:

₹10,00,000 = ₹4,00,000 + ₹6,00,000

Real-Life Practice

Every company balance sheet follows this equation.

13. Accounting Period

Meaning

Specific period for preparing accounts.

Examples

  • Monthly
  • Quarterly
  • Yearly

Real Practice

Most companies prepare annual financial statements from:

1 April to 31 March (India).

14. Voucher

Meaning

Document supporting a transaction.

Examples

  • Invoice
  • Receipt
  • Bill
  • Payment slip

Real Example

Whenever a company pays rent, a rent receipt acts as a voucher.

15. Invoice

Meaning

Document issued by seller to buyer.

Contains

  • Quantity
  • Price
  • GST
  • Total amount

Real-Life Example

Every online purchase from Amazon generates an invoice.

16. Journal

Meaning

Book of original entry where transactions are first recorded.

Example

Purchased furniture for cash ₹20,000.

Furniture A/c Dr ₹20,000
To Cash A/c ₹20,000

17. Ledger

Meaning

Book where journal entries are classified account-wise.

Example

All cash transactions go into Cash Account.

All furniture transactions go into Furniture Account.

Real Practice

Modern accounting software automatically creates ledgers.

18. Trial Balance

Meaning

Statement prepared to verify arithmetic accuracy.

Formula

Total Debit = Total Credit

Example

If debit side = ₹5,00,000

Credit side = ₹5,00,000

Trial Balance agrees.

19. Financial Statements

These are final reports prepared by businesses.

Main Statements

  1. Trading Account
  2. Profit & Loss Account
  3. Balance Sheet
  4. Cash Flow Statement

Real Practice

Public companies publish these annually for investors.

Practical Real-World Illustration

Imagine you start a small stationery shop.

Transaction Accounting Term
Invest ₹2,00,000 Capital
Buy stock ₹1,00,000 Purchases
Sell stock ₹1,50,000 Sales
Shop furniture ₹20,000 Asset
Borrow ₹50,000 from bank Liability
Pay rent ₹10,000 Expense
Customer owes ₹5,000 Debtor

Using these terms correctly helps prepare accurate financial statements and measure business performance.

Conclusion

Understanding accounting terminology is the first step toward mastering accounting. In real businesses—from a small kirana store in Mumbai to large companies like Reliance Industries or Tata Consultancy Services—these terms are used daily to record transactions, prepare reports, calculate profits, manage cash flow, and make business decisions. Mastery of these fundamentals makes advanced topics such as journal entries, GST accounting, final accounts, and financial statement analysis much easier to learn.
