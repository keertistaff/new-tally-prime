Introduction

Every business performs transactions such as purchasing goods, selling products, paying salaries, receiving money from customers, paying rent, and many more.

Before these transactions are recorded in books of accounts, they must be analyzed and converted into proper accounting entries. This process is called Journalising.

In traditional accounting, transactions are first recorded in the Journal Book.

In TallyPrime, journalising is done automatically when voucher entries are passed, but understanding journal entries is extremely important because:

  • It helps identify Debit and Credit accounts.
  • It improves accounting accuracy.
  • It helps avoid wrong voucher entries.
  • It builds a strong accounting foundation.

Learning Objectives

After studying this chapter, students will be able to:

  • Understand Journalising
  • Identify accounts involved in transactions
  • Apply Golden Rules of Accounting
  • Pass Journal Entries
  • Record transactions correctly in TallyPrime
  • Understand practical business scenarios

What is Journalising?

Journalising is the process of recording business transactions in chronological order using Debit and Credit rules.

A journal entry shows:

  • Which account is Debited
  • Which account is Credited
  • Amount involved
  • Narration (reason for transaction)

Definition

A Journal is known as the:

"Book of Original Entry"

because every transaction is first recorded here before posting to ledger accounts.

Format of Journal Entry

Date Particulars L.F. Debit Amount Credit Amount
xx Account Dr. xxx
To Account xxx
(Narration)

Steps in Journalising

For every transaction:

Step 1

Identify the accounts involved.

Step 2

Determine the type of account.

  • Personal Account
  • Real Account
  • Nominal Account

Step 3

Apply Golden Rule.

Step 4

Determine Debit and Credit.

Step 5

Pass Journal Entry.

Example 1 – Started Business with Cash

Mr. Raj starts a business with ₹1,00,000 cash.

Analysis

Accounts involved:

  1. Cash A/c
  2. Capital A/c

Types

Cash → Real Account

Capital → Personal Account

Golden Rule

Real Account:

Debit What Comes In

Cash comes into business.

Personal Account:

Credit the Giver

Owner gives capital.

Journal Entry

Cash A/c Dr.              ₹1,00,000
     To Capital A/c              ₹1,00,000

(Being capital introduced into business)

Real-Life Use Case

A shop owner deposits personal savings into the business bank account.

This transaction occurs when:

  • Opening a new store
  • Starting a startup
  • Beginning a consultancy business

Example 2 – Purchased Furniture for Cash

Furniture purchased for ₹20,000 cash.

Accounts

  • Furniture A/c
  • Cash A/c

Types

Furniture → Real Account

Cash → Real Account

Rule

Furniture comes in → Debit

Cash goes out → Credit

Journal Entry

Furniture A/c Dr.         ₹20,000
      To Cash A/c                ₹20,000

(Being furniture purchased for cash)

Real-Life Use Case

Office purchases:

  • Chairs
  • Tables
  • Office Cabinets
  • Workstations

Example 3 – Purchased Goods on Credit

Goods purchased from Ramesh ₹50,000.

Accounts

  • Purchase A/c
  • Ramesh A/c

Types

Purchase → Nominal

Ramesh → Personal

Rule

Expense/Purchase → Debit

Creditor (giver) → Credit

Journal Entry

Purchase A/c Dr.          ₹50,000
      To Ramesh A/c             ₹50,000

(Being goods purchased on credit)

Business Practice

Most wholesalers purchase goods on credit and pay suppliers later.

Examples:

  • Grocery stores
  • Hardware shops
  • Medical stores

Journal Entry for GST Purchase

Goods purchased: ₹50,000

GST @18% = ₹9,000

Total = ₹59,000

Entry

Purchase A/c Dr.         ₹50,000
Input CGST A/c Dr.        ₹4,500
Input SGST A/c Dr.        ₹4,500
      To Supplier A/c            ₹59,000

(Being goods purchased with GST)

Journal Entry for GST Sales

Goods sold: ₹30,000

GST @18% = ₹5,400

Total = ₹35,400

Entry

Customer A/c Dr.         ₹35,400
      To Sales A/c              ₹30,000
      To Output CGST A/c         ₹2,700
      To Output SGST A/c         ₹2,700

(Being goods sold with GST)

Common Journalising Mistakes

1. Confusing Debtor and Creditor

Wrong:

Sales A/c Dr.

Correct:

Customer A/c Dr.
To Sales A/c

2. Debiting Income

Income should generally be credited.

Example:

Commission Received

Correct:

Cash A/c Dr.
To Commission A/c

3. Crediting Expenses

Expenses must be debited.

Wrong:

To Salary A/c

Correct:

Salary A/c Dr.
To Cash A/c

Shortcut Formula for Journalising

Ask three questions:

Question 1

What account is affected?

Question 2

Is it Personal, Real, or Nominal?

Question 3

Which Golden Rule applies?

Then pass the entry.

Journalising and Voucher Entry in TallyPrime

Suppose salary ₹15,000 is paid.

Journal Entry

Salary A/c Dr.      ₹15,000
      To Cash A/c         ₹15,000

In TallyPrime:

Gateway of Tally → Vouchers → Payment Voucher (F5)

Enter:

  • Salary A/c → Debit ₹15,000
  • Cash A/c → Credit ₹15,000

TallyPrime automatically records the journal entry in the background.
